Complete Technical Architecture

1st Year B.Tech Project · Implementing Camgöz et al. (2020) · Dataset: iSign (IIT Kanpur)

1. Project Overview & iSign Dataset

This project implements the Sign Language Transformer architecture from the paper "Sign Language Transformers: Joint End-to-end Sign Language Recognition and Translation" by Camgöz et al. (CVPR 2020).

Instead of processing raw video (which is slow and introduces bias based on signer appearance), the system converts video into skeletal coordinates (pose) using MediaPipe and feeds these coordinates to an encoder-decoder transformer.

Dataset Details

  • Dataset: iSign (largest ISL dataset, released 2024 by IIT Kanpur).
  • Total Pairs: 118,228 video-sentence pairs extracted from 6,058 unique YouTube videos.
  • Pose Dimension: 576 keypoints × 3 coordinates (x,y,z) = 1,728 dimensions per frame.
  • Vocabulary: Capped at the top 8,000 most frequent words (plus special tokens like <unk>) to ensure stable learning from 118k samples.

From a raw video to an English sentence in five stages:

🦴

03 — NORMALISE
📊

Per-sequence mean and std normalisation. Removes signer position bias.

🤖

56.7M parameter transformer reads the sequence and generates English words.
05 — OUTPUT

Beam search (width=5) decodes the most probable English sentence.

5. Debugging & Key Insights

Vocabulary Capping

The raw iSign dataset contained 69,806 unique words. This caused a critical training failure as the softmax over 69k classes was impossible to learn from only 118k examples. The fix was to cap the vocabulary to the top 8,000 most frequent words and replace the rest with an <unk> token, covering ~95% of the data and jump-starting BLEU scores.

CUDA Out of Memory

Processing up to 1778 frames required a 1778² × 512 attention matrix per sample. We resolved OOM errors by reducing the batch size to 16 and using gradient accumulation steps of 2, preserving the effective batch size of 32.

The BLEU Score Ceiling

The original paper notes that SLT translates isolated sentences cut from continuous videos. Sign language relies heavily on co-referencing (spatial reference points) introduced in earlier sentences. Without this broader video context, translation quality encounters a hard natural ceiling, an open research problem as of 2024.
